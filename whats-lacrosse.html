01 Origins

A Brief History of Lacrosse

Lacrosse is one of the oldest team sports in the world. Long before European settlers arrived in North America, Indigenous peoples — including the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois), Ojibwe, and Cherokee — were playing a form of the game they called Tewaarathon, meaning “little brother of war.”

The game was deeply spiritual, played to resolve conflicts, train warriors, and honour the Creator. Matches could last days, involve hundreds of players, and span miles of terrain. The stick, with its distinctive netted head, remains largely unchanged in concept today.

French missionaries and colonists encountered the game in the 17th century. They named it “la crosse” — the cross — after the resemblance of the stick to a bishop’s staff. The sport was codified in the 19th century and became an Olympic event in 1904 and 1908.

02 Europe

Lacrosse in Europe

Lacrosse arrived in Europe in the late 19th century, brought by British students who had encountered the game in Canada. England became the first European nation to play organised lacrosse, with the English Lacrosse Association founded in 1892.

For much of the 20th century, lacrosse remained a niche sport in Europe — primarily played in the United Kingdom, Ireland, and a handful of universities across the continent. The turning point came in the 1990s and 2000s, as American students studying abroad brought equipment and enthusiasm to new countries.

Today, the European Lacrosse Federation (ELF) represents over 30 nations. Germany, France, the Netherlands, Italy, and the Czech Republic have all developed competitive club structures, youth academies, and national leagues. The 2028 Olympics — which will feature Sixes lacrosse — has given the sport a significant boost in visibility and investment across the continent.

03 Formats

Three Ways to Play

Lacrosse is played in three distinct formats, each with its own pace, rules, and appeal. Click to explore each one.

Field 🎯

Field Lacrosse

Field lacrosse is the traditional outdoor form of the game and the most widely played internationally. Contested on a grass or turf pitch roughly the size of a football field, it’s a full-contact sport combining the speed of hockey, the physicality of rugby, and the precision of basketball.

Each team fields 10 players including a goalkeeper. The objective is to shoot a small rubber ball into the opposing team’s goal using a long-handled stick with a netted head. Games are fast, physical, and require exceptional athleticism and hand-eye coordination.

Field lacrosse is the format most commonly played at club and national level across Europe, and the primary discipline at the World Lacrosse Championship.

Sixes ⏬

Sixes Lacrosse

Sixes is the newest and most dynamic format of lacrosse, purpose-built for modern audiences and compact venues. Developed by World Lacrosse and adopted as the Olympic format for the 2028 Los Angeles Games, Sixes plays fast — very fast.

With only six players per side on a smaller field, the game is relentless. A 30-second shot clock keeps teams under constant pressure. There are fewer stoppages, more goals, and a pace of play closer to basketball or hockey than traditional field lacrosse.

Sixes is rapidly growing in Europe, particularly attracting younger players and new fans who discover the sport through Olympic coverage. It’s also proving to be an excellent development format for youth players.

Box 🏠

Box Lacrosse

Box lacrosse — also called indoor lacrosse — was born in Canada in the 1930s when ice hockey rinks were converted during summer months. Today it’s a sport in its own right, played in enclosed arenas on a small turf surface surrounded by dasher boards.

The enclosed space creates a completely different dynamic: faster transitions, more physical contact, higher-scoring games, and a unique set of technical skills. Players use shorter sticks with deeper pockets, and goalkeepers wear heavy padding that covers most of the net.

Box lacrosse is particularly popular in Canada and the Haudenosaunee nations, but is growing steadily in Europe — especially in countries like Germany, Austria, and Switzerland where indoor facilities are abundant during winter months.
